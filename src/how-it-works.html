How Memoro Vault Works

Reclaiming Ownership in a World of Exposure
In today’s digital world, personal data is not owned—it is extracted. Every search, backup, login, and cloud sync becomes part of a surveillance economy. Most people do not control their data. They rent access to it from companies who monetize it, mismanage it, or lose it entirely.

The rise of decentralized technologies has created new forms of digital value—cryptocurrency wallets, identity keys, encrypted archives—but the means of preserving them remain fragile. A lost password, corrupted drive, or compromised platform can erase everything.

Memoro Vault changes that.
It enables anyone to encrypt critical files, instructions, and credentials into a portable, tamper-evident vault that can be stored anywhere and unlocked only through exact memory-based knowledge.

There are no servers, no logins, and no trusted intermediaries. Just a sealed vault file and a recovery program that runs entirely offline.

The Problem Memoro Vault Solves

Access to long-term preservation is uneven. Some individuals rely on legal structures—trusts, foundations, estate attorneys—to transfer intent and assets across generations. Most do not. When someone passes away or loses capacity, accounts and data often remain locked behind proprietary systems or forgotten credentials.

The problem extends far beyond cryptocurrency:

  • Family records lost in cloud accounts or inaccessible formats
  • Journalists and whistleblowers needing offline, tamper-proof storage
  • Parents wishing to pass on private messages, credentials, or creative work
  • Refugees or stateless persons needing durable access to identity documents

Memoro Vault allows users to define the conditions for recovery with cryptographic precision. You can split knowledge across multiple trusted people—ensuring no one can access the vault alone, but collaboration enables recovery.

Memory-Based Encryption

During creation, users define between 9 and 25 custom memory questions. These can be personal, obscure, or collaboratively known. Answers are never stored. Instead, they are used to derive a cryptographic key using Argon2, a modern memory-hard key derivation function designed to resist brute-force attacks—even with specialized hardware.

Layered Access Control

Phase One: Initial Gatekeeping
The first two answers act as a strict gate. Each incorrect attempt triggers an exponentially increasing lockout, slowing down brute-force attempts. No hints or feedback are given.

Phase Two: Permutation-Based Recovery
Once Layer One is passed, the user must enter exactly the correct answers in the correct order. Memoro Vault stores the required answer sequence as a cryptographic hash. Even if an attacker knows all correct answers, they must still guess the correct permutation..

This makes brute-force infeasible. Instead of wasting CPU cycles on proof-of-work, attackers face a combinatorial explosion of possible valid sequences—an elegant shift from compute-bound to knowledge-bound security.

To make the vault accessible to the right people, and to prevent self-lockout:

  • Optional character or letter hints may be revealed by the vault builder.
  • Red herring ("trap") questions can be included to confuse adversaries
  • All answers are forced lowercase and trimmed to prevent unintentional lockouts

File and Environment Security

  • All files are encrypted using AES-GCM with 256-bit keys
  • Metadata, filenames, and final messages are encrypted
  • Vaults are output as tamper-evident, portable ZIP files
  • The vault cannot be recovered if the vault file is altered.

Decryption only succeeds if the exact vault structure, cryptographic parameters, and answer order are preserved. This binds each vault to its original interface without revealing any secrets.

Flexible Distribution

Memoro Vault supports a wide range of secure sharing strategies:

  • Keep it private and fully self-recoverable
  • Distribute questions among trusted collaborators
  • Store vaults publicly (USB, IPFS, cloud)—without risk of exposure

There is no backdoor. Without the exact answer sequence and matching vault version, recovery is mathematically impossible.
